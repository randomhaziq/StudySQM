## Introduction
A Quality Management System (QMS) is a structured framework of processes, standards, procedures, and responsibilities used to ensure software meets customer expectations, complies with standards, and achieves consistent quality.
>A system that defines how an organisation plans, assures, controls, and improves quality.

## Purpose of Quality Management System (QMS)
A QMS aims to:
- Ensure consistent quality in software development
- Define processes, standards, and responsibilities
- Improve customer satisfaction
- Minimize defects, rework, and cost
- Increase team efficiency and predictability
- Support continuous improvement (Kaizen)

## QMS in Software vs Traditional Manufacturing
1. Software QMS emphasises:
   - Process maturity (CMMI, SPICE/ISO 15504)
   - Defect prevention and detection techniques
   - Agile quality practices (pair programming, CI/CD)
   - Testing strategies (unit, integration, system)
   - Documentation and traceability

2. Manufacturing QMS focuses more on physical product consistency.

## Standards Related to QMS
1. ISO 9001
   - General QMS standard used across industries.

2. ISO/IEC 25010
   - Defines software product quality characteristics.

3. ISO/IEC 15504 (SPICE)
   - Evaluates process capability and maturity.

4. CMMI
   - Capability Maturity Model for improving development processes.

5. ISO/IEC 12207
   - Software lifecycle processes.

>QMS integrates these standards to form a quality framework.

## Tools and Techniques in QMS
- Reviews (walkthroughs, inspections, technical reviews)
- Testing & verification
- Metrics (defect density, DRE, reliability metrics)
- Risk management
- Configuration management tools (Git, Jenkins)
- CI/CD pipelines
- Audits and compliance checks

## QMS Metrics
Quality management relies on measurable indicators, including:
- Defect density
- Defect removal efficiency (DRE)
- MTTF, MTTR
- Code coverage
- Review effectiveness
- Test pass/fail ratio
- Process compliance ratio
- Customer satisfaction metrics

## Benefits of Implementing QMS
Expected outcomes:
- Higher-quality software
- Fewer defects early and late
- Reduced development cost and time
- Increased customer satisfaction
- Predictable, repeatable processes
- Improved team communication
- Better risk and change management
- Support for audits, certifications, and contracts

## Common QMS Problems
- Over-documentation
- Lack of team training
- Process followed only for audits
- No continuous improvement
- Poor integration with Agile
- Metrics misused to punish developers
